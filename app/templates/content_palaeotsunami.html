What is a Palaeotsunami?

A palaeotsunami is a tsunami that occurred in a place before written records existed at that location.

How can we tell there's been a palaeotsunami?

Evidence for palaeotsunamis commonly takes the form of unusual sediment deposits found in diverse coastal areas. These deposits can range from boulders to fine mud, and often fine inland in response to diminishing wave energy. Other forms of evidence include changes in geochemistry (e.g. some sediment layers may be saltier than might otherwise be expected), microfossils (e.g. the unusual presence of deep sea fossils found on land) and archaeological information (e.g. settlements destroyed by extreme events such as a palaeotsunami). Scientists seek to identify, map and date such deposits to build a record and understanding of palaeotsunamis through time.

What do we look for?

More than 30 criteria are currently used to recognise palaeotsunamis, although most studies will use just a few to support their interpretations. The more characteristics we can recognise, the more confident we can be that they were left by a palaeotsunami. A date is rarely however defined to a specific year. Rather, an approximation of the age of an event generally improves as more evidence becomes available from other sites. In contrast, historical tsunamis refer to events that have happened since people have been around to write down details and they are usually talked about in terms of when they happened (e.g. the Chilean tsunami in May 1960).

How simple is it?

In spite of our improving understanding about palaeotsunamis there are many factors which complicate our interpretations. Coastlines may have moved over the hundreds or thousands of years since the tsunami happened, so a given site may now be closer or further away from the sea than it was when the tsunami occurred. Sediment layers can also be washed away by floods, erosion, human activities and even more recent tsunamis. A lack of evidence, however, does not rule out past (or future) tsunamis along a stretch of coast. While it might not always be possible to tell exactly when a palaeotsunami happened or how far it travelled across the sea, learning about such events is critical for understanding ongoing risks.
